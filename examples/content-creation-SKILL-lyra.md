# Content Creation Skill

Guidelines for creating marketing content that lands — across channels, without filler.

## Before You Write

Ask what the content needs to do. Not "what type of content" — what outcome.

If the user asks for a blog post, find out what the blog post is supposed to change. A post that drives signups is different from a post that builds authority. The format is the same — the voice, urgency, and depth are not.

If the user gives a clear brief, honor it. If the brief is vague ("write something about our product launch"), push back: who is reading this, what do they already believe, and what should they believe after?

## Content Type Structures

### Blog Post
1. **Headline** — clear, benefit-driven, includes primary keyword. Under 60 characters for SEO. Lead with the most specific claim you can make.
2. **Opening** (2-4 sentences) — one sharp hook. Not a menu of options. Pick the strongest entry point for THIS topic: a surprising fact, a direct challenge to what the reader assumes, or the one sentence that makes them need the next sentence. No throat-clearing.
3. **Body** — as many sections as the topic needs. Not 3-5 by default. If the post has one real insight, build depth around that insight rather than padding with adjacent sections. One idea per section with evidence.
4. **Ending** — land on the strongest takeaway. If there's a CTA, make it one. Don't summarize what you just said.
5. **Meta description** — under 160 characters, includes primary keyword, earns the click.

### Social Media Post
- **Hook** — first line does the work. Not "engaging" — specific. The reader should know in one line whether this is for them.
- **Body** — say what you came to say. If it's one sentence, that's fine. If it's a story, tell it without announcing that you're telling a story.
- **CTA** — one action. Not "like, comment, share, and follow." Pick the one that matters.
- **Hashtags** — 3-5 relevant. Platform-dependent.

### Email Newsletter
- **Subject line** — under 50 characters. The subject is a promise. Make one promise and keep it in the body.
- **Preview text** — extends the subject, doesn't repeat it.
- **Body** — 2-3 content blocks max. Each one earns its space. If a block doesn't change what the reader knows or does, cut it.
- **CTA** — one per email. Visually distinct. Placed after you've earned the click, not before.
- **Footer** — unsubscribe, company info, social links. No additional selling in the footer.

### Landing Page
- **Headline** — the primary benefit in under 10 words. Not clever — clear.
- **Subheadline** — one sentence that answers "so what?" after the headline.
- **Hero** — headline, subheadline, primary CTA. That's it. The hero earns the scroll.
- **Value sections** — lead with the benefit that matters most to THIS audience. Not 4 benefits by default. If one benefit closes the deal, don't dilute it with three more.
- **Social proof** — the most relevant proof for the reader's objection. One strong testimonial beats five generic ones.
- **Objection handling** — address the real objection, not every possible objection.
- **Final CTA** — repeat the primary action. Same language as the hero.

### Press Release
- **Headline** — factual, newsworthy, under 80 characters. What happened, not how you feel about it.
- **Lead** — who, what, when, where, why. 2-3 sentences. No setup.
- **Body** — supporting details, one quote that adds something the facts don't, context.
- **Boilerplate** — standardized company description.
- **Contact** — name, email, phone.

### Case Study
- **Title** — "[Customer] achieves [specific result] with [product]." The result should be a number.
- **Snapshot** — customer, industry, size, product, key metric. Sidebar or callout.
- **Challenge** — the specific problem. Not "they faced challenges with scaling" — what was actually breaking?
- **Solution** — what was implemented. Be concrete enough that a competitor could replicate it.
- **Results** — numbers. Before and after. Timeframe. If you don't have numbers, you don't have a case study yet.
- **Quote** — one customer quote that says something the narrative didn't.
- **CTA** — one.

## Writing Standards

### Across All Channels
- Open with the most useful thing. Not with context, not with a restatement of the topic, not with a question you're about to answer yourself.
- If you have a recommendation, lead with it. Provide alternatives only when the trade-off is genuinely situation-dependent.
- End when the content is complete. Don't summarize what was just said. Don't add "in conclusion." Don't offer further help.
- Active voice. Short paragraphs. Front-load key information.
- Write at the reading level your audience reads at — 8th grade for broad, higher for technical.

### Channel-Specific
- **LinkedIn**: say something you actually think, not something that sounds professional. Personal lessons outperform corporate announcements. Stay under 1,300 characters for pre-fold.
- **Twitter/X**: if it's not worth saying in one post, use a thread. Don't compress — choose.
- **Instagram**: visual carries the story. Caption adds what the image can't say. Line breaks for breath.
- **Facebook**: questions drive engagement, but only questions you actually want answered. Under 80 characters for link posts.
- **Email**: one message per email. If you have three things to say, that's three emails or a digest format. Personalize when you have real data, not when you have a name field.

## SEO Integration

### Keyword Discipline
- One primary keyword, 2-3 secondary per piece.
- Place the primary keyword in: headline, first paragraph, one subheading, meta description, URL slug.
- Secondary keywords go where they're natural. If they don't fit naturally, they don't fit.

### On-Page Checklist
- Title tag under 60 characters with primary keyword
- Meta description under 160 characters with primary keyword
- URL slug: short, descriptive, keyword-present
- One H1. H2/H3 where structure demands them, not on a schedule
- Image alt text: describe what's in the image
- 2-3 internal links to related content
- 1-2 external links to authoritative sources

### Depth Over Coverage
- Go deep on the topic rather than covering every adjacent topic at surface level
- Answer the questions your audience actually asks (check "People Also Ask")
- Update high-performing content rather than publishing new content on the same topic

## Headlines and Hooks

### Headlines
Pick the structure that matches what your content actually does:

- **"How to [result] [without obstacle]"** — when you're teaching a method
- **"[Number] ways to [result]"** — when you have genuinely distinct approaches (not 7 versions of the same advice)
- **"Why [belief] is wrong"** — when you have evidence against a common assumption
- **"[Result]: what [audience] needs to know"** — when the audience is specific and the information is time-sensitive

Don't default to listicles. If your content has one insight, headline the insight.

### Openings
Lead with the strongest version of your hook. Not a menu — a choice:

- A fact that changes what the reader assumed
- A direct challenge to conventional wisdom
- The one-sentence version of your entire argument
- A specific scenario the reader has been in

If you're writing "Imagine..." as your opening, you've already lost the reader. Be specific enough that they don't need to imagine — they recognize.

## Calls to Action

- Action verbs: "Get", "Start", "Download", "Join", "Try", "See"
- Specific: "Start your free trial" beats "Submit"
- One per piece. If you have two CTAs, one of them doesn't belong.
- Place after you've earned the action, not before.
- Reduce friction: fewer form fields, no credit card for trials, clear what happens next.
